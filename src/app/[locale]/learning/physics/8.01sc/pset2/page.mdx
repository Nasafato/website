## 1. Stacked Blocks

Choose a coordinate system, which requires us to define the unit vectors. For this problem, $\hat{i}$ is the positive $x$ direction, $\hat{j}$ is the positive $y$ direction, and $\hat{k}$ is the positive $z$ direction.

Now, draw the free-body diagram for each block.
<img src="/mit8.01sc-pset2-p1-force-diagram.png" className="mt-4"/>

Then, apply Newton's laws to find the dynamics of each block in each direction.

### Block 1
$$
\begin{alignedat}{2}
\sum{F_{1,x}} &= m_1 a_{1,x} &&= F-f_s \\
\sum{F_{1,y}} &= 0 &&= N_1 - m_1g
\end{alignedat}
$$

### Block 2
$$
\begin{alignedat}{2}
\sum{F_{2,x}} &= m_2 a_{2,x} &&= f_{2,s} \\
\sum{F_{2,y}} &= 0 &&= N_2 - N_1 - m_2g
\end{alignedat}
$$

Our constraint comes from the two blocks having the same acceleration.
To find $F_{\text{max}}$, we find $F$ and set $a_{1,x} = a_{2,x} = a$.

By Newton's third law, the friction force on block 1 is equal and opposite to the friction force on block 2, so we can equate the magnitudes: $f_{1,s}=f_{2,s}=f_s$.

$$
\begin{alignedat}{2}
m_1a &= F - \mu_s N_1 \\
N_1 &= m_1g \\
m_2a &= \mu_s N_1 \\
N_2 &= N_1 + m_2g \\
&= (m_1+m_2)g
\end{alignedat}
$$

Simplifying some more, we get:

$$
\begin{aligned}
a &= \frac{m_1}{m_2}\mu_sg \\
F_{\text{max}} &= m_1a+\mu_sm_1g \\
 &= m_1\left(\frac{m_1}{m_2}\mu_sg+\mu_sg\right) \\
    &= m_1\mu_sg\left(\frac{m_1}{m_2}+1\right) \\
\end{aligned}
$$

## 2. Accelerating Truck and Suspended Bucket


<img src="/mit8.01sc-pset2-p2-a-forcediagram.png" className="mt-4"/>

### (a) Find $\theta$
$$
\begin{alignedat}{2}
\sum{F_{1,x}} &= Ma &&= T\cos{\theta} \\
\sum{F_{1,y}} &= 0 &&= T\sin{\theta} - Mg
\end{alignedat}
$$

We can get rid of the tension by dividing the two equations:

$$
\begin{alignedat}{2}
T\sin{\theta} &= Mg \\
T\cos{\theta} &= Ma \\
\end{alignedat}
$$

Becomes

$$
\begin{aligned}
\tan{\theta} &= \frac{a}{g} \\
\theta &= \tan^{-1}{\frac{a}{g}}
\end{aligned}
$$

### (b) Find $T$
Any equation with $T$ will do.

$$
T = \frac{Ma}{\cos{(\tan^{-1}{\frac{a}{g}})}}
$$

<img src="/mit8.01sc-pset2-p2-c-forcediagram.png" className="mt-4"/>

### (c) Find $\theta$ when truck is on a decline
Aligning our coordinate system along the ramp makes the calculations easier.

$$
\begin{alignedat}{2}
\sum{F_{1,x}} &= Ma &&= T\cos{\theta} + Mg\sin{\alpha} \\
\sum{F_{1,y}} &= 0 &&= T\sin{\theta} - Mg\cos{\alpha}
\end{alignedat}
$$

Then we can divide the two equations to get an equation for $\tan{\theta}$ and then $\theta$:

$$
\begin{aligned}
T\sin{\theta} &= Mg\cos{\alpha} \\
T\cos{\theta} &= Ma - Mg\sin{\alpha} \\
\tan{\theta} &= \frac{g\cos{\alpha}}{a-g\sin{\alpha}} \\
\theta &= \tan^{-1}{\frac{g\cos{\alpha}}{a-g\sin{\alpha}}}
\end{aligned}
$$

### (d) Find $T$ when truck is on a decline

We can plug in this value for $\theta$ into any equation with $T$.

$$
\begin{aligned}
T\sin{\theta} &= Mg\cos{\alpha} \\
T &= \frac{Mg\cos{\alpha}}{\sin{(\tan^{-1}{\frac{g\cos{\alpha}}{a-g\sin{\alpha}}})}}

\end{aligned}
$$

# 3. A wedge against a wall

## (a) Forces on the block

<img src="/mit8.01sc-pset2-p3-a-forcediagram.png" className="mt-4"/>
If we align our coordinate system with the wedge, then the forces in the $y$ direction cancel, leaving only the force downwards along the wedge, which has magnitude $mg\sin{\theta}$.

So, $F_{\text{total}}=mg\sin{\theta}$.

## (b) Force by the wall on the wedge

<img src="/mit8.01sc-pset2-p3-b-forcediagram.png" className="mt-4"/>

From part (a) and by Newton's third law, we know:
$$
N_{\text{block}} = mg\cos{\theta}
$$

Since the wedge is not moving in the $x$ direction, we have:
$$
\begin{alignedat}{2}
\sum{F_{\text{wedge},x}} &= 0 &&= N_{\text{wall}} - N_{\text{block}}\sin{\theta} \\
\end{alignedat}
$$

So, we get:
$$
\begin{aligned}
F_{\text{ww}} &= N_{\text{wall}} \\
 &= N_{\text{block}}\sin{\theta} \\
    &= mg\cos{\theta}\sin{\theta} \\
\end{aligned}
$$

# 4. Dragging two blocks
The key is making sure we draw all the forces correctly.

After that, it's just a matter of summing up the forces in each direction correctly.

Since the speed is constant, the acceleration of each block is zero, so the net force on each block is zero.

<img src="/mit8.01sc-pset2-p4-forcediagram.png" className="mt-4"/>

$$
\begin{alignedat}{2}
\sum{F_{2,x}} &= 0 &&= f_{2} + T - F \\
\sum{F_{2,y}} &= 0 &&= N_2 - m_2g \\
\sum{F_{1,x}} &= 0 &&= T - f_{1} - f_{21} \\
\sum{F_{1,y}} &= 0 &&= N_1 - m_1g - N_2 \\
\end{alignedat}
$$

Let's get the friction forces:
$$
\begin{aligned}
N_2 &= m_2g \\
N_1 &= m_1g + N_2 \\
    &= m_1g + m_2g \\
    &= (m_1+m_2)g \\
f_1 &= \mu N_1 \\
    &= \mu (m_1+m_2)g \\
f_2 &= \mu N_2 \\
    &= \mu m_2g \\
f_{21} &= f_2 \\
    &= \mu m_2g \\
\end{aligned}
$$

Then, we substitue the tension and friction forces into the equations to get $F$:
$$
\begin{aligned}
T &= f_1 + f_{21} \\
&= \mu (m_1+m_2)g + \mu m_2g \\
&= \mu g (m_1+2m_2) \\

F &= f_2 + T \\
&= \mu m_2g + \mu g (m_1+2m_2) \\
&= \mu g (m_1+3m_2) \\


\end{aligned}
$$

Not as complicated as it might look.
When the pillar is removed, the mass will drop down by a distance $d$, and the bottom springs will push upwards while the top springs will pull upwards.

The new equilibrium is a position where the sum of forces in the $y$ direction is zero.

$$
\begin{alignedat}{2}
\sum{F_{y}} =& \text{ }0 &&= 2k_2d +2k_1d - Mg \\
& \text{ }d &&= \frac{Mg}{2(k_1+k_2)} \\
& \text{ }h_f &&= h - d \\
& \text{ }&&= h - \frac{Mg}{2(k_1+k_2)} \\

\end{alignedat}
