In these problems, we introduce additional constraints in the form of a pulley and string. The constant length of the string lets us relate the motion of different parts of the system to one another.

## 1. Block and Pulley System


### (a) Block barely sliding up incline
In this case, the static friction force acts downwards along the incline to resist the block's upward motion.

$$
\begin{alignedat}{2}
\sum{F_{1,x}} = & \text{ } 0 &&= T - f_s - mg \sin{\theta} \\
& T &&= f_s + mg\sin{\theta} \\
&  &&= \mu_s mg\cos{\theta} + mg\sin{\theta} \\
\sum{F_{1,y}} = & \text{ } 0 &&= N - mg \cos{\theta} \\
& N &&= mg \cos{\theta} \\

\sum{F_{2,y}} = &\text{ } 0 &&= 2T - m_2 g \\
 & m_2 && = \frac{2T}{g} \\
 & m_{2,\text{min}} && = 2m_1(\mu_s \cos{\theta} + \sin{\theta}) \\

\end{alignedat}
$$

### (b) Block barely sliding down incline
Static friction force acts upwards along the incline now.

$$
\begin{alignedat}{2}
\sum{F_{1,x}} = & \text{ } 0 &&= T + f_s - m_1g \sin{\theta} \\
 & T &&= m_1g\sin{\theta} - f_s \\
 &  &&= m_1g\sin{\theta} - \mu_s m_1g\cos{\theta}  \\
\sum{F_{1,y}} = & \text{ } 0 &&= N - m_1g \cos{\theta} \\
& N &&= m_1g \cos{\theta} \\

\sum{F_{2,y}} = &\text{ } 0 &&= 2T - m_2 g \\
 & m_2 && = \frac{2T}{g} \\
 & m_{2,\text{max}} && = 2m_1(\sin{\theta} - \mu_s \cos{\theta}) \\

\end{alignedat}
$$

### (c) Block sliding upwards

This time, the blocks have non-zero acceleration, and the kinetic friction force acts downwards along the incline.

$$
\begin{alignedat}{2}
\sum{F_{1,x}} = & m_1 a_{1,x} &&= T - f_s - m_1g \sin{\theta} \\
\sum{F_{1,y}} = & \text{ } 0 &&= N - m_1g \cos{\theta} \\
\sum{F_{2,y}} = &\text{ } m_2 a_{2,y} &&= 2T - m_2 g \\

\end{alignedat}
$$

We'll need an additional constraint condition, now that we've introduced a new unknown, and we can get the condition from the string length equations.

At a glance, we expect $m_1$ to move twice as quickly as $m_2$. We can show this explicitly by first writing the length equation for the string, then taking the derivative of it and relating the parts to each other. We know the derivative of the length sums to zero, even if the individual parts of the string may change.

We call $l_1$ the segment from $m_1$ to the first pulley, $s$ the segment from the ceiling to the height of the first pulley, and $l_2$ the length of the two segments between the height of the first pulley and the second pulley, which are equal.

$$
\begin{aligned}
L &= l_1 + l_2 + l_2 + s \\
\frac{dL}{dt} &= \frac{dl_1}{dt} + 2\frac{dl_2}{dt} \\
\frac{d^2L}{dt^2} &= \frac{d^2l_1}{dt^2} + 2\frac{d^2l_2}{dt^2} \\
0 &= \frac{d^2l_1}{dt^2} + 2\frac{d^2l_2}{dt^2} \\
\frac{d^2l_1}{dt^2} &= -2\frac{d^2l_2}{dt^2} \\
a_{1,x} &= -2a_{2,y} \\
a_{2,y} &= -\frac{a_{1,x}}{2}
\end{aligned}
$$

So, we have the following four equations, and now we can solve for $a_{1,x}$.
$$
\begin{aligned}
a_{2,y} &= -\frac{a_{1,x}}{2} \\
m_1 a_{1,x} &= T - f_s - m_1g \sin{\theta} \\
0 &= N - m_1g \cos{\theta} \\
m_2 a_{2,y} &= 2T - m_2 g \\

\end{aligned}
