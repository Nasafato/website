## 1. Bead on a Rotating Hoop

### (a) Finding equilibrium $\omega$
<img src="/mit8.01sc-pset3-p1-a-forcediagram.png" className="mt-4"/>

There is a certain angular velocity at which the normal force from the ring on the bead netted with the gravitational pull on the bead equals zero.

Note the rotation is around the axis, so the radial distance is $R\sin{\theta}$, not $R$.

$$
\begin{alignedat}{2}
\sum{F_{r}} = & -mR\sin{\theta}\omega^2 && = -N\sin{\theta} \\
            & N\sin{\theta} && =   \\
\sum{F_{y}} = & \text{ } 0 && = N\cos{\theta} - mg \\
            & \text{ } N && = \frac{mg}{\cos{\theta}} \\

\end{alignedat}
$$

With this, we can find $\omega$:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\omega^2 &= \frac{N}{R} \\
         &= \frac{g}{R\cos{\theta}} \\
\omega   &= \sqrt{\frac{g}{R\cos{\theta}}}
\end{aligned}
$$

### (b) Finding $\omega_0$
If $\theta=0$, $\cos{\theta}=1$, and we have:
$$
\omega_0=\sqrt{\frac{g}{R}}
$$

## 2. Banked Turn
The direction of the friction force will differ when calculating $v_{\text{min}}$ vs. $v_{\text{max}}$. At $v_{\text{min}}$, the car is about to slip downwards towards in the center of the circle, so the friction force is pointed in the positive $\hat{r}$ direction.

At $v_{\text{max}}$, the car is about to slip upwards towards the outside of the circle, so the friction force is pointed in the negative $\hat{r}$ direction.

<img src="/mit8.01sc-pset3-p2-forcediagram.png" className="mt-4"/>

Remember that $r\omega = v$.

### Calculating $v_{\text{min}}$:
$$
\begin{alignedat}{2}
f_{s,\text{min}} = & \text{ } \mu N \\
\sum{F_{r}} = & -\frac{mv^2}{R} && = f_{s,\text{min}}\cos{\beta} - N\sin{\beta}  \\
& -\frac{mv^2}{R} && = N(\mu \cos{\beta} - \sin{\beta}) \\
& \text{ } v^2 && = \frac{NR}{m}(\sin{\beta} - \mu \cos{\beta}) \\

\sum{F_{y}} = & \text{ } 0 && = N\cos{\beta} + f_{s,\text{min}}\sin{\beta} - mg \\
            &  && = N\cos{\beta} + \mu N{\beta} - mg \\
            & && = N(\cos{\beta} + \mu\sin{\beta}) - mg \\
            & mg && = N(\cos{\beta} + \mu\sin{\beta}) \\

\end{alignedat}
$$

Dividing the two equations, we get:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{v^2}{mg} &= \frac{R(\sin{\beta} - \mu \cos{\beta})}{m(\cos{\beta} + \mu\sin{\beta})} \\
v^2 &= \frac{Rg(\sin{\beta} - \mu \cos{\beta})}{\cos{\beta} + \mu\sin{\beta}} \\

v_{\text{min}} &= \sqrt{\frac{Rg(\sin{\beta} - \mu \cos{\beta})}{\cos{\beta} + \mu\sin{\beta}}}

\end{aligned}
$$

### Calculating $v_{\text{max}}$:

We just need to change the direction of the friction force, so we get:

$$
\begin{alignedat}{2}
f_{s,\text{max}} = & \text{ } \mu N \\
\sum{F_{r}} = & -\frac{mv^2}{R} && = -f_{s,\text{max}}\cos{\beta} - N\sin{\beta}  \\
& \frac{mv^2}{R} && = N(\mu \cos{\beta} + \sin{\beta}) \\
& \text{ } v^2 && = \frac{NR}{m}(\mu \cos{\beta} + \sin{\beta}) \\

\sum{F_{y}} = & \text{ } 0 && = N\cos{\beta} - f_{s,\text{max}}\sin{\beta} - mg \\
            &  && = N\cos{\beta} - \mu N{\beta} - mg \\
            & && = N(\cos{\beta} - \mu\sin{\beta}) - mg \\
            & mg && = N(\cos{\beta} - \mu\sin{\beta}) \\
\end{alignedat}
$$

Dividing the two equations, we get:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{v^2}{mg} &= \frac{R(\mu \cos{\beta} + \sin{\beta} )}{m(\cos{\beta} - \mu\sin{\beta})} \\
v^2 &= \frac{Rg(\mu \cos{\beta} + \sin{\beta})}{\cos{\beta} - \mu\sin{\beta}} \\

v_{\text{max}} &= \sqrt{\frac{Rg(\mu \cos{\beta} + \sin{\beta})}{\cos{\beta} - \mu\sin{\beta}}}

\end{aligned}
$$

## 3. Tetherball Breaking Off

### (a) Find $\omega$
We'll need Newton's equations to find $\omega$.

$$
\begin{alignedat}{2}
\sum{F_{r}} = & -ml\sin{\beta}\omega^2 && = -T\sin{\beta} \\
\sum{F_{y}} = & \text{ } 0 && = T\cos{\beta} - mg \\
            & \text{ } mg && = T\cos{\beta} \\
\end{alignedat}
$$

Dividing the two equations, we get:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{l\omega^2}{g} &= \frac{1}{\cos{\beta}} \\
\omega^2 &= \frac{g}{l\cos{\beta}} \\
\omega &= \sqrt{\frac{g}{l\cos{\beta}}}
\end{aligned}
$$

### (b) Find $d$, the distance traveled after breaking off
This is now just kinematics.

First, the only horizontal velocity it has is its tangential velocity at the time of breaking off. The direction doesn't matter for our purposes, only the magnitude.

Second, we know the only acceleration in the vertical direction is from gravity and from this we can figure out $t$, how long it takes the ball to hit the ground.

Then, $d$ equals $v_{\text{tangential}}t$.

$$
\begin{aligned}
v_{\text{tangential}} &= l\sin{\beta}\omega = l\sin{\beta}\sqrt{\frac{g}{l\cos{\beta}}} = \sin{\beta}\sqrt{\frac{gl}{\cos{\beta}}} \\
a_{\text{vertical}}(t) &= g \\
v_{\text{vertical}}(t) &= -gt \\
z_{\text{vertical}}(t) &= h - \frac{1}{2}gt^2 \\
\end{aligned}
$$

Setting the vertical height to zero and moving terms around, we get:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{1}{2}gt^2 &= h \\
t &= \sqrt{\frac{2h}{g}} \\
d &= v_{\text{tangential}}t  \\
&= \sin{\beta}\sqrt{\frac{gl}{\cos{\beta}}} \sqrt{\frac{2h}{g}} \\
&= \sin{\beta}\sqrt{\frac{2hl}{\cos{\beta}}}
\end{aligned}
$$


## 4. Two Boxes Around a Shaft

### (a) Find $T_B$
$$
\begin{alignedat}{2}
\sum{F_{r}} = & -2m_2d\omega^2 && = T_B \\
\end{alignedat}
$$

### (b) Find $T_A$
$$
\begin{alignedat}{2}
\sum{F_{r}} = & -m_1d\omega^2 && = T_B - T_A \\
              & \text{ } T_A && = T_B + m_1d\omega^2 \\
              & \text{ } T_A && = 2m_2d\omega^2 + m_1d\omega^2 \\
              & &&= d\omega^2(2m_2 + m_1) \\
\end{alignedat}
$$

## 5. Satellite

### (a) Which satellite has higher speed?
$$
\begin{alignedat}{2}
\sum{F_{A,r}} = & -\frac{m_Av_A^2}{r_A} && = -\frac{Gm_Em_A}{r_A^2} \\
              & \text{ } v_A^2 && = \frac{Gm_E}{r_A} \\
\end{alignedat}
$$

By similar logic for $B$, $v_B^2 = \frac{Gm_E}{r_B}$.

Clearly, since $r_A > r_B$, $v_A < v_B$. The farther a satellite is from the Earth, the slower it moves.

### (b) Which satellite has a longer period $T$?

The period is the time it takes for a satellite to make one revolution around earth, so $\omega T = 2\pi$. Remember radians are unit-less.

$$
\begin{aligned}
\omega_A T_A &=  2 \pi \\
T_A &= \frac{2\pi}{\omega_A} \\
T_B &= \frac{2\pi}{\omega_B} \\
\end{aligned}
$$

Now, we must compare angular velocities. From part (a), we can convert velocity to angular velocity for each satellite:

$$
\begin{aligned}
v_A^2 &= \frac{Gm_E}{r_A} \\
\omega_A^2 r_A^2 &= \frac{Gm_E}{r_A} \\
\omega_A^2 &= \frac{Gm_E}{r_A^3} \\
\omega_B^2 &= \frac{Gm_E}{r_B^3}
\end{aligned}
$$

Since $r_A > r_B$, $\omega_A < \omega_B$, which implies $T_A > T_B$.

## 6. A coin on a rotating disk

<img src="/mit8.01sc-pset3-p6-forcediagram.png" className="mt-4"/>

### (a) Force of static friction on the coin

Newton's laws equations:
$$
\begin{alignedat}{2}
f_{s} = & \text{ } \mu N \\
\omega = & \text{ } \alpha t \\
\sum{F_{r}} = & -md\omega^2 && = f_{s} \\

\sum{F_{y}} = & \text{ } 0 && = N - mg \\
            & N && = mg \\

\end{alignedat}
$$

We get $f_s = -md\alpha^2 t^2$.

### (b) Angular velocity at which coin starts to slip
The maximum static friction force is limited by the coin's weight and the coefficient of static friction. We know $N = mg$, so the maximum static friction force is $\mu_s N = \mu_s mg$.

Using our radial equation, we get:
$$
\begin{aligned}
-md\omega^2 &= \mu_s mg \\
\omega^2 &= \frac{\mu_s g}{d} \\
\omega &= \sqrt{\frac{\mu_s g}{d}}
\end{aligned}
$$
